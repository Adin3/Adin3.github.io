01 THE CORE PROBLEM & OUR SOLUTION

In Cyberpunk 2020 and Cyberpunk RED, there is exactly one Netrunner role. Every character who jacks into the NET does the same thing with the same skill set. D&D 5e, however, has over a dozen spellcasting classes and subclasses, each with a different flavor for how they access magic.

Our campaign already agreed that "magic" is "netrunning/quickhacking" and "spells" are "quickhacks." The problem is that not every spellcaster should feel like a full netrunner. A Barbarian with a couple of quickhack grenades is not the same thing as someone who was raised in a deep-dive server farm.

This document establishes a five-tier classification system that maps every 5e spellcasting class to a Cyberpunk NET archetype, drawing on lore from Cyberpunk 2013/2020/RED, Cyberpunk 2077, Edgerunners, and the broader world to justify each placement. The goal: every player at the table understands what their character is in this world, without argument.

02 THE TIER SYSTEM — MASTER CLASSIFICATION TABLE

Tier NET Archetype 5e Classes Key Stat Cyberpunk Equivalent
TIER 1 True Netrunner / Chair Runner Wizard INT The classic Netrunner role. Full NET access, deep-dive capable, writes/modifies their own quickhacks. Think Bartmoss, Alt Cunningham, Spider Murphy.
TIER 2 Field Netrunner / Combat Hacker Artificer, Eldritch Knight (Fighter), Arcane Trickster (Rogue) INT Runs the NET but stays mobile. Combat-capable with a cyberdeck. Think Lucy (Edgerunners), T-Bug in a firefight, 2077's V with quickhacks + guns.
TIER 3 Cyberware-Integrated / Daemon-Linked Warlock, Sorcerer CHA NET access through implanted systems or rogue AI patron links, not learned skill. Think Soulkiller victims, AI-touched individuals, Cyberpsychos with daemon-integrated chrome, people connected to entities beyond the Blackwall.
TIER 4 Techie / Support Ops / Rockerboy Cleric, Druid, Bard, Ranger WIS / CHA Access to quickhacks through external systems (corpo support networks, gang-linked infrastructure, or specialist tech) — or, in the Bard's case, not quickhacks at all but subliminal influence through music and sound. Not netrunners — more like Techs, Medtechs, Rockerboys, or Fixers who operate through entirely different vectors.
TIER 5 Quickhack User / Chrome Operator Paladin, Ranger (low level), Eldritch Knight (low level) Various Carries a few quickhacks loaded onto cyberware, like a Solo with a Militech Paraline. Not a netrunner in any meaningful sense — they're combat operatives who happen to have a couple of combat hacks on their rig.
Key principle: Tiers 1–2 are "Netrunners" in the world. Tier 3 is something weirder and rarer. Tiers 4–5 are not netrunners — they use quickhacks like tools, the way a Solo might use a grenade. Nobody calls a Solo a "grenadier" because they threw a frag.

03 CLASS-BY-CLASS BREAKDOWN & LORE JUSTIFICATIONS

WIZARD NETRUNNER

TIER 1 — TRUE NETRUNNER / CHAIR RUNNER

Why Tier 1: The Wizard is the only 5e class that learns magic through rigorous study, writes spells into a spellbook, and relies entirely on Intelligence. This is a one-to-one match for how netrunning works in every edition of Cyberpunk. Netrunners learn programming, study ICE architecture, and build their quickhack libraries from scratch.

Lore anchor — Rache Bartmoss: The most infamous netrunner in Cyberpunk history was a reclusive, physically frail genius who spent years studying NET architecture and writing programs. He created the DataKrash that destroyed the old NET in the 2020s. He didn't have innate talent — he had obsessive intelligence and thousands of hours of study. Pure wizard energy.
Lore anchor — Alt Cunningham: A brilliant programmer who created Soulkiller through pure technical genius. She was kidnapped by Arasaka specifically because of her learned expertise. Her power came from study and invention, not innate gifts.

In-game role: Full NET access, can deep-dive (chair run), write/modify quickhacks, break ICE, interact with AI constructs. The party's primary netrunner. Spell preparation = loading quickhacks onto your cyberdeck before a run.

ARTIFICER NETRUNNER TECH

TIER 2 — FIELD NETRUNNER / TECHIE-HACKER HYBRID

Why Tier 2: Artificers build things. They infuse objects with magic, create gadgets, and use INT. In Cyberpunk, this is a Techie who also runs the NET — someone who builds their own cyberdeck, modifies their own chrome, and writes quickhacks as part of a broader engineering skillset. They're netrunners, but their identity isn't just netrunning.

Lore anchor — 2077 Techie concept: Early Cyberpunk 2077 development had a Techie class alongside the Netrunner. The Artificer fills this gap — someone who hacks and builds. Think of a ripperdoc who also runs a sideline cracking Militech firmware.

In-game role: Can net-dive with limitations. Excels at crafting cyberware, modifying equipment, and deploying hardware-based quickhacks (turrets, drones, embedded tech). Infusions = cyberware installations.

ARCANE TRICKSTER (ROGUE) NETRUNNER HYBRID

TIER 2 — FIELD NETRUNNER / INFILTRATOR-HACKER

Why Tier 2: This is the exact Lucy archetype from Edgerunners. A rogue who also netrunns. Not a chair runner — a field operative who jacks in on-site during infiltration, hacks security systems mid-heist, and can hold their own in a fight. They learned netrunning (INT-based, like a Wizard) but their primary identity is the infiltration, stealth, and close-quarters work.

Lore anchor — Lucy (Edgerunners): Raised in an Arasaka deep-dive training facility as a child. Escaped. Became a pickpocket and street kid first, then leveraged her netrunning skills as one tool among many. She doesn't sit in a chair — she runs the NET on a rooftop in the middle of a job. She's fast, lethal, and technical. This is the Arcane Trickster.
Lore anchor — T-Bug (Cyberpunk 2077): While T-Bug was more of a remote support runner, her work on the Konpeki Plaza heist shows the field-netrunner model — hacking security in real-time to support operatives on the ground. An Arcane Trickster who emphasizes the "trickster" less and the "arcane" more would resemble T-Bug closely.

In-game role: Can net-dive but prefers quick, targeted hacks during physical operations. Sneak Attack = precision neural strikes or security exploits. Mage Hand = remote drone control or ghost-hacking nearby devices. Their netrunning complements their infiltration, not the other way around.

ELDRITCH KNIGHT (FIGHTER) SOLO NETRUNNER-LITE

TIER 2 — COMBAT NETRUNNER / SOLO WITH A DECK

Why Tier 2: A soldier or merc who picked up enough netrunning to integrate quickhacks into combat. INT-based casting means they actually learned this, putting them above the "innate" casters. But their spell list is very limited — they're running combat quickhacks off a stripped-down military cyberdeck.

Lore anchor — Militech Combat Netrunners: Militech (who runs Night City in 2190) is known for training military-grade combat netrunners — soldiers who can hack enemy systems mid-firefight. These aren't the brainy deep-divers of Arasaka's old NetWatch division. They're warriors with a cyberdeck bolted to their kit.

In-game role: Limited quickhack library (the Fighter's small spell list). Excels at combat hacks: short-circuiting enemy cyberware, weapon jamming, sensory overloads. Cannot deep-dive or do serious NET exploration without extensive downtime effort.

WARLOCK DAEMON-LINKED

TIER 3 — CYBERWARE-INTEGRATED / AI PACT

Why Tier 3: Warlocks don't learn magic — they get it from a patron. In Cyberpunk terms, this is someone whose netrunning capability comes from an external entity: a rogue AI beyond the Blackwall, a corpo black-project implant, a daemon that's taken up residence in their neural architecture, or an experimental Soulkiller variant that left something behind.

They cast using CHA, not INT. They didn't study for this. Something gave it to them, and it probably wants something in return.

Lore anchor — Rogue AIs / The Blackwall: After Bartmoss's DataKrash, rogue AIs evolved beyond the Blackwall — a digital barrier maintained by NetWatch. In 2077, we see these AIs making contact with humans (the Voodoo Boys' attempts, the Peralez mind-control subplot). A Warlock's "patron" is one of these entities — an intelligence beyond the wall that has lent part of itself to a human host.
Lore anchor — Alt Cunningham (post-Soulkiller): After her body died, Alt became a digital entity beyond the Blackwall. By 2077 she's something alien and vast. A Warlock could be someone Alt — or something like her — has touched.

In-game role: Their "quickhacks" are more like daemon invocations. They can interface with the NET, but it's unsettling — their methods look wrong to trained netrunners. Limited slots that recharge quickly (Pact Magic = a daemon rebooting their access). Eldritch Blast = a neural spike powered by their patron's malware.

Patron mapping:

Warlock PatronCyberpunk Equivalent

FiendA malicious rogue AI beyond the Blackwall. Demands data, access, or acts of digital sabotage in exchange for power.

SORCERER INNATE HACKER

TIER 3 — CYBERWARE-INTEGRATED / BORN DIFFERENT

Why Tier 3: Sorcerers have innate magic — they were born with it or something changed them fundamentally. In Cyberpunk, this is someone whose biology was altered — experimental in-utero cyberware, genetic modification from a corpo black project, exposure to a rogue AI in childhood, or a botched Soulkiller process that left them with abilities no one can explain.

They use CHA, not INT. They don't understand how they do what they do. They just... can.

Lore anchor — Deep-dive farm survivors: Both Arasaka and Militech ran programs where children were forced into deep-dive training to create netrunners. Most died or were broken. A Sorcerer could be a survivor who came out changed — their neural pathways rewired at a fundamental level. They don't run programs; their brain is the program.
Lore anchor — Cyberpsychosis as metamagic: Sorcerous metamagic maps eerily well to pushing cyberware beyond safe limits. Twinned Spell = overclocking a neural processor. Subtle Spell = a hack so clean it leaves no trace. Heightened Spell = redlining your cyberdeck at the risk of neural burnout. The Sorcerer's "sorcery points" are essentially their Humanity meter.

CLERIC SUPPORT OPS

TIER 4 — CORPO NETWORK OPERATOR / MEDTECH-HACKER

Why Tier 4: Clerics get their power from a divine source and use WIS. In Cyberpunk, this translates to someone who accesses a curated quickhack network provided by an organization — a corpo, a gang, a government, or an ideology. They don't write their own code. They download approved packages from their "domain."

Lore anchor — Corpo Net-Ops: Every megacorp has support staff who run proprietary quickhack suites. A Militech field medic with a medical quickhack suite, a Trauma Team operator with combat-triage software, an Arasaka security tech with surveillance daemons — these are all "Clerics" in the sense that their power comes from their organizational access, not personal skill.

DRUID SUPPORT OPS NOMAD-TECH

TIER 4 — NOMAD TECH-SHAMAN / SCAVENGER-HACKER

Why Tier 4: Druids connect to nature and shapeshift. In a Cyberpunk world, "nature" is the old NET — the wild, unregulated infrastructure that still exists in the wastelands beyond city limits. Druids are Nomad techs who interface with derelict NET architecture, scavenge pre-DataKrash systems, and use jury-rigged equipment that corpo netrunners would consider primitive.

Lore anchor — Nomad Clans: Nomad families in Cyberpunk maintain their own independent tech infrastructure. They don't have access to corpo networks, but they've adapted. Wild Shape = jacking into drone bodies or repurposed security bots via a Nomad-rigged interface.

BARD ROCKERBOY FIXER

TIER 4 — ROCKERBOY / FIXER / MEDIA

Why Tier 4: Bards use CHA and are generalists — performers, social engineers, and influencers. In Cyberpunk, the primary match is the Rockerboy — someone who moves people through music, presence, and sheer force of personality. Their "quickhacks" aren't code at all. They're subliminal messages woven into sound: frequencies that trigger emotional responses through cyberware audio processors, encoded data buried in a bassline, vibrations that resonate with neural implants in ways corpo engineers never intended.

Lore anchor — Johnny Silverhand: The quintessential Rockerboy. Silverhand didn't hack systems with a cyberdeck — he hacked people with a guitar and a microphone. His music started riots, shifted public opinion, and brought a megacorp to its knees. Bardic Inspiration isn't a quickhack upload; it's a riff so good it makes your chooms fight harder.

In-game role: Bardic Inspiration = a killer track that pumps up allies. Charm/enchantment spells = subliminal frequencies targeting cyberware audio implants. Healing spells = music that triggers the body's endorphin response and syncs with medical cyberware. Countersong = jamming hostile frequencies. They may have minor NET literacy (enough to broadcast, hijack speakers, or piggyback signals), but they are not netrunners by any stretch — they're something older and arguably more dangerous.

RANGER SOLO

TIER 4–5 — SOLO / NOMAD SCOUT WITH BASIC QUICKHACKS

Why Tier 4–5: Rangers are wilderness experts who get a small amount of spellcasting. In Cyberpunk, this is a Solo or Nomad scout running a minimal quickhack loadout — a few combat utilities, maybe a diagnostic suite, nothing fancy. They're in the field, not the NET.

Lore anchor — Panam Palmer / Nomad scouts: Skilled fighters and survivalists who use tech as a tool but would never call themselves netrunners. Their "spells" = field-deployed quickhack grenades, cyberware-integrated scanners, and basic electronic warfare countermeasures.

PALADIN SOLO

TIER 5 — CORPO ENFORCER / MILITECH OPERATIVE WITH EMBEDDED QUICKHACKS

Why Tier 5: Paladins are warriors with a cause and minor divine magic. In Cyberpunk, this is a dedicated corpo enforcer or ideological operative (Militech black-ops, a devout Nomad clan guardian, a MaxTac officer) with quickhacks baked into their military-grade cyberware. They didn't learn hacking. Their Sandevistan came preloaded with a combat OS.

Lore anchor — Adam Smasher / MaxTac Officers: Full-borg combat units with integrated systems that include basic electronic warfare. Smite = channeling your cyberware's combat suite into a devastating attack. Nobody is calling Adam Smasher a "netrunner," even though his borg body runs more code than most cyberdecks.

04 MULTICLASS & HYBRID NETRUNNERS

Multiclassing is where this system really shines, because Cyberpunk characters are rarely one thing. Here's how to think about it:

Multiclass Effective Tier Cyberpunk Identity
Rogue / Wizard 1–2 Deep-dive-trained infiltrator. The Lucy archetype. A true netrunner who operates in the field. More Wizard levels = more chair time; more Rogue levels = more field time. Either way, a legitimate netrunner.
Fighter / Wizard 2 Militech combat netrunner. A soldier who went through NET training. Dangerous in a firefight AND on the NET.
Warlock / Rogue 2–3 An operative with an AI parasite giving them abilities. They don't fully understand their own hacking — it comes through the patron.
Sorcerer / Warlock 3 Something deeply wrong happened to this person. Born with neural anomalies AND contacted by a rogue AI. They're more daemon than human when they jack in.
Paladin / Warlock 3–5 A corpo enforcer whose loyalty earned them a patron's "gift" — an AI embedded in their combat rig. They serve two masters.
Cleric / Wizard 1–2 A corpo-trained netrunner with organizational backing. Has both personal skill AND access to a curated network. Dangerous combination.
Bard / Rogue 4 Classic Fixer. Social engineering, contacts, and just enough NET literacy to be useful. Not a netrunner — a people runner.

06 DM RULINGS & TABLE AGREEMENTS

Suggested rulings to prevent future arguments:

Ruling 1 — Netrunner Identity Is Not Class-Locked: Any character with INT-based spellcasting (Wizard, Artificer, Eldritch Knight, Arcane Trickster) is a trained netrunner. The extent of their NET capabilities scales with their spellcasting level, not their class.
Ruling 2 — Deep-Diving vs. Field Running: Deep-diving (full immersion chair running) requires at minimum 3rd-level Wizard spell slots. Field running (quick hacks during physical operations) is available to anyone with spellcasting and a cyberdeck (arcane focus).
Ruling 3 — CHA-Based Casters Are Not Traditional Netrunners: Warlocks, Sorcerers, and Bards access quickhacks through non-standard means (patron links, innate mutations, social engineering). They can interact with the NET, but trained netrunners (INT casters) will notice their methods are "off." This is a flavor distinction, not a mechanical penalty.
Ruling 4 — Multiclass Netrunning: A multiclass character's netrunning capability is determined by their highest INT-casting class level. A Rogue 7 / Wizard 3 is a Tier 2 netrunner with 3rd-level netrunning capability but 7th-level field skills. A Rogue 3 / Wizard 7 is essentially a Tier 1 netrunner who happens to be good with a knife.
Ruling 5 — Cyberdecks Are Arcane Foci: Every spellcasting focus in 5e is a cyberdeck variant. A Wizard's spellbook = their cyberdeck's program library. A Cleric's holy symbol = their corpo-issued terminal. A Warlock's focus = the daemon interface embedded in their chrome. A Druid's totem = a Nomad-rigged jury-built deck.
A Note on Backstory Validation: If a player has written a backstory that involves netrunner training (like growing up in a deep-dive farm), that backstory should be respected mechanically. The class/multiclass they chose is how they're expressing that backstory in the 5e system. Telling a Rogue/Wizard that their Wizard levels "don't count" as real netrunning invalidates their character concept and the effort they put into it. The tier system above gives every class a place — use it to say "yes, and" instead of "no, but."
