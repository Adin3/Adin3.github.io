01 OVERVIEW NETRUNNING CRAFTING

"Your own code is the only code you can trust. Any gonk can buy a quickhack off the street, but a real netrunner writes their own. It's slower, it's expensive, and it's yours."

Quickhack Programming is the process of writing, compiling, and permanently installing a quickhack (spell) onto your cyberdeck. Unlike buying a pre-made quickhack or finding one in the field, programming your own means it's tailored to your deck architecture and becomes a permanent part of your library — no charges, no expiration.

This system is based on 5e's spell scroll crafting rules, reflavored for the Cyberpunk setting: faster turnaround, heavier eddie cost, and a skill-check system that lets experienced netrunners and hackers cut costs through expertise.

Core Principle: Programming a quickhack = scribing a spell to your spellbook / learning a new spell. Once it's on your deck, it's permanent. You know it. You can prepare it. It's yours.

02 REQUIREMENTS RULES

Who Can Program Quickhacks?

Any character with spellcasting can program quickhacks from their own class spell list. You cannot program quickhacks from another class's list unless you have access to it through multiclassing or a feature that grants you spells from that list.

What Do You Need?

A Cyberdeck: You must have a cyberdeck installed. No deck, no programming.

The Spell on Your Class List: The quickhack must be a spell that exists on your class's spell list. You don't need to already know it — that's the whole point.

Spell Slot Access: You must be able to cast spells of that level. A 3rd-level Wizard can't program a 5th-level quickhack.

Eddies: The primary cost. See the table below.

Time: Programming takes downtime. Faster than traditional scroll crafting, but not instant.

Tools: Access to your cyberdeck and a workspace (your own shop, a rented terminal, a friendly netrunner's den, etc). Nothing exotic — just somewhere you can sit and code without getting shot.

Class List Only: A Wizard can only program spells from the Wizard spell list. A Cleric programs from the Cleric list. A Rogue/Wizard multiclass can program from the Wizard list (since Arcane Trickster spells are drawn from the Wizard list with restrictions). If your DM rules that a spell isn't appropriate for the setting, that overrides the class list.

04 THE PROGRAMMING PROCESS RULES

Programming a quickhack follows three steps:

Step 1 — Declare & Pay

Tell the DM which quickhack you're programming. Confirm it's on your class spell list and that you can cast spells of that level. Pay the full base cost in eddies upfront (or the discounted cost, if you passed the Optimization Check first — see Section 05).

Step 2 — Spend the Time

Programming happens during downtime. You need access to your cyberdeck and a workspace. You can split the time across multiple days. Other downtime activities can be done in the remaining hours of each day, but you can't program two quickhacks simultaneously.

Step 3 — Compile & Install

At the end of the programming time, the quickhack is permanently added to your spell list. You know it. You can prepare it normally (or it's always available, depending on your class). No further cost, no charges, no expiration. It's part of your deck now.

No Failure State: If you meet the requirements and pay the cost, the programming succeeds. You don't roll to see if it works. The Optimization Check (below) is optional and only affects price — it doesn't risk the quickhack itself.

05 OPTIMIZATION CHECKS (DISCOUNT SYSTEM) CRAFTING

"Optimization is what separates a script kiddie from a real programmer. Anyone can copy-paste code. A real coder knows where to cut corners without cutting throats."

Before paying the base cost, a character can attempt an Optimization Check — a series of ability checks representing their ability to write efficient code, reuse existing libraries, and find shortcuts in the programming process. Success reduces the eddie cost. Failure doesn't prevent crafting — you just pay full price.

How It Works

Before starting programming, make one to three checks (your choice how many you attempt). Each check targets a different aspect of the process. You can attempt any combination, in any order, but each check can only be attempted once per quickhack.

The Three Optimization Checks

CheckAbility / SkillWhat It RepresentsDCDiscount on Success
Code Efficiency INT (Arcana) or INT (Hacking, if your table uses it) Writing cleaner, leaner code that uses fewer resources to compile. 10 + Quickhack Level −15% cost
Architecture Familiarity INT (Investigation) or Proficiency with Cyberdeck (Tinker's Tools equivalent) Knowing your deck's architecture well enough to skip redundant compile steps. 12 + Quickhack Level −15% cost
Black Market Sourcing CHA (Persuasion) or CHA (Streetwise, if your table uses it) Finding cheaper code libraries, bootleg compilers, or a friend who owes you a favor. 13 + Quickhack Level −20% cost

Stacking Discounts

Discounts stack additively. If you pass all three checks, you get −50% off the base cost. That's the maximum discount possible.

Pass 1 check: −15% to −20% off.

Pass 2 checks: −30% to −35% off.

Pass all 3: −50% off (the cap).

Failing a check has no penalty. You just don't get that discount. You can still program the quickhack at full price (or at whatever discount your other checks earned). This system rewards expertise without punishing anyone for trying.
The DCs scale with quickhack level, meaning low-level hacks are easy to optimize while high-level ones require serious skill investment. A Level 1 quickhack has DCs of 11/13/14 — very achievable. A Level 9 quickhack has DCs of 19/21/22 — only the best in Night City are shaving costs off those.

06 COLLABORATIVE PROGRAMMING NETRUNNING CRAFTING

"Never underestimate a compile party. Two decks are better than one, three's a crowd — just don't let the Cleric touch the runtime environment."

Quickhack programming doesn't have to be a solo job. Spellcasters can assist each other in the coding process — sharing the workload, lending expertise on checks, and cutting down programming time. The quickhack still only goes onto one character's deck, and they still need it on their own class list to actually cast it. But getting there can be a team effort.

Who Can Help: Any spellcaster classified as Tier 1, 2, or 3 on the Netrunner Classification system can assist with collaborative programming. This includes Wizards, Artificers, Eldritch Knights, Arcane Tricksters, Warlocks, and Sorcerers. Tier 4–5 casters (Clerics, Druids, Bards, Rangers, Paladins) can receive help but cannot provide it — their relationship with quickhacks is too different from actual code to be useful in the programming process.

Assistance Benefits

1. Split the Time

Each additional Tier 1–3 programmer helping reduces the total programming time. Divide the base time by the number of programmers working on it (minimum 1 hour per level of the quickhack).

2 programmers: Time ÷ 2

3 programmers: Time ÷ 3

Maximum of 3 total programmers (including the primary crafter). More people on the same codebase just creates merge conflicts.

2. Help on Optimization Checks

An assistant can use the Help action on one of the three Optimization Checks, granting the primary programmer advantage on that check. Each assistant can only Help on one check per quickhack. If there are two assistants, they can Help on two different checks.

The assistant must be proficient in the relevant skill to grant advantage (standard 5e Help rules).

3. Second Opinion (Optional)

If the primary programmer fails an Optimization Check, one assistant may attempt the same check themselves at the same DC as a “second opinion.” If they pass, the discount is applied. This can only happen once per quickhack — you get one backup attempt, not infinite retries.

Class Restriction Still Applies: Collaboration doesn't bypass class lists. A Wizard can help a Cleric program "Revivify" faster and cheaper — the Wizard handles the code architecture while the Cleric handles the medical logic — but the Wizard cannot then cast Revivify themselves. It goes on the Cleric's deck only.

A Field Netrunner (Rogue/Wizard) helps a Corpo Medtech (Cleric) program "Spirit Guardians" (Level 3) as "Perimeter Daemon Swarm."

Spirit Guardians is on the Cleric spell list. The Field Netrunner can't cast it, but as a Tier 2 netrunner they can assist with the coding.

Base cost: 7,500eb. Base time: 24 hours (3 days).

With the Field Netrunner helping: Time ÷ 2 = 12 hours (1.5 days).

The Medtech attempts Optimization Checks. The Field Netrunner uses Help on the Code Efficiency check (proficient in Arcana), granting advantage.
• Code Efficiency (DC 13, with advantage): Medtech rolls 9 and 16. Takes the 16. Pass. −15%.
• Black Market Sourcing (DC 16): Medtech rolls 12. Fail. The Field Netrunner uses their Second Opinion — rolls CHA (Persuasion) at DC 16. Gets a 17. Pass. −20%.

Final cost: 7,500 − 35% = 4,875eb. Done in 1.5 days instead of 3. The Medtech's deck now has Perimeter Daemon Swarm. The Field Netrunner helped write the code but can't run the program themselves.
Collaborative programming is meant to encourage teamwork during downtime and give Tier 1–3 characters a way to support the whole party's capabilities, not just their own. It also gives hybrid archetypes like the Field Netrunner (Rogue/Wizard) a natural role as a “quickhack consultant” — they may not have the highest-level spells themselves, but they can make everyone else's programming faster and cheaper.

07 EXAMPLES RULES

A Field Netrunner (Rogue 5 / Wizard 3) wants to program "Misty Step" (Level 2) as "Optic Reboot + Sprint."

Misty Step is on the Wizard spell list. The Field Netrunner has 3 Wizard levels, so they can cast 2nd-level spells. They qualify.

Base cost: 3,000eb. Time: 16 hours (2 days).

They attempt two Optimization Checks:
• Code Efficiency (DC 12): INT (Arcana) check. Rolls a 15. Pass. −15%.
• Architecture Familiarity (DC 14): INT (Investigation) check. Rolls an 11. Fail. No discount.

Final cost: 3,000 − 15% = 2,550eb. Optic Reboot + Sprint is programmed over two days of downtime.
A Corpo Medtech (Cleric, Level 9) wants to program "Revivify" (Level 3) as "Emergency Neural Reboot."

Revivify is on the Cleric spell list. The Medtech is 9th level. Qualifies easily.

Base cost: 7,500eb. Time: 24 hours (3 days).

They attempt all three checks:
• Code Efficiency (DC 13): Rolls 18. Pass. −15%.
• Architecture Familiarity (DC 15): Rolls 16. Pass. −15%.
• Black Market Sourcing (DC 16): Rolls 14. Fail.

Final cost: 7,500 − 30% = 5,250eb.
A Chair Runner (Wizard, Level 17) programs "Wish" (Level 9) as "Blackwall Override."

Base cost: 300,000eb. Time: 120 hours (15 days). This is a serious investment.

They attempt all three checks:
• Code Efficiency (DC 19): Rolls 22. Pass. −15%.
• Architecture Familiarity (DC 21): Rolls 24. Pass. −15%.
• Black Market Sourcing (DC 22): Rolls 19. Fail.

Final cost: 300,000 − 30% = 210,000eb. Still absurdly expensive. Programming a Level 9 quickhack should feel like a milestone, not a shopping trip.

08 QUICK REFERENCE RULES

Quickhack Programming — At a Glance

Who: Any spellcaster. Own class spell list only.

What: Program a spell you don't know onto your cyberdeck. It becomes permanent.

Cost: 500eb (cantrip) to 300,000eb (9th level). See table.

Time: 4 hours (cantrip) to 15 days (9th level).

Discount: Up to 3 Optimization Checks before programming. Max −50% off.

Limit: No cap on total quickhacks. If you have the eddies and the time, go wild.

Failure: None. If you qualify and pay, it works. Optimization Checks only affect price.

Requirements: Cyberdeck installed, workspace access, appropriate spell level access.

This system is intentionally eddies-heavy to keep it balanced. A Level 5 quickhack at 30,000eb is a real investment — roughly the cost of a Sandevistan. Players should feel the weight of choosing to spend resources on expanding their library versus buying new chrome or gear. The Optimization Checks reward characters who invest in INT and social skills, which fits the netrunner/hacker fantasy perfectly.
